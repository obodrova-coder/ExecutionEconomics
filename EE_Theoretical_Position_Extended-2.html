Preface

This monograph combines a body of work developed over several years to address the persistent problem of why established firms and organisations often fail to execute projects and adapt to new technology despite ample information and resources. Decisions are not carried through, reforms stall, pilots fail and strategic shifts do not translate into sustained outcomes, allowing problems to escalate rather than resolve. This work argues that the puzzle may be better understood by reference to institutional decision sovereignty.

The term decision sovereignty describes the capacity of individuals, commercial companies and public agencies to execute, revise and sustain decisions to completion under conditions of uncertainty, contestation and stress. While leadership talent is not in short supply, deployable decision sovereignty remains a scarce and precious commodity, constrained by human cognitive limits and institutional design.

This manuscript synthesises diverse concepts of convex governance costs, organisational responses to neurological constraints, the "uncontested territory" of innovation-driven organisational spaces separated from legacy structures, the Second Track process and decision sovereignty into the Decision Sovereignty Framework — a coherent analytical framework for examining institutional execution. The paper presents formal expressions for authority, control, execution-relevant information and veto exposure, together with a framework for assessing whether projects appear feasible or constrained, and whether alternative architectures or pathways may be required.

The Core Argument

Recent technological innovations, most notably artificial intelligence powered by large language models, were expected to reduce administrative friction and the cost of accurate prediction in public agencies and established firms. However, despite the increasing adoption of AI models and processes, productivity and institutional performance have shown limited or uneven improvement in Australia and across much of the developed world.

A much-cited MIT report found that only 5% of 300 public AI deployments in 2025 improved results, with the other 95% mired in "adoption without transformation". An alternative interpretation is that current difficulties reflect longer-standing constraints in the ability to carry major decisions through to completion. Outmoded systems of governance are simply overwhelmed when new capabilities expand the scope and volume of actionable choices.

The binding constraint in modern economies is no longer information, but the capacity to execute decisions under complexity. The Decision Sovereignty Framework formalises this constraint through the equation Y = d(P) × S, where d(P) is decision quality enabled by predictive capacity P, and S is decision sovereignty — a signed, real-valued transmission operator.

The Missing Variable

Execution Economics surveys twenty-five adjacent traditions across six scholarly families, and extends its analysis to non-economic disciplines including game theory, complexity science, control theory, jurisprudence, military doctrine, philosophy of action, distributed computing and decision neuroscience. Three structural patterns recur across all traditions:

First, every tradition measures unsigned magnitudes and cannot distinguish mission-aligned from mission-extractive institutional capacity. Second, no tradition formalises the joint structure linking decision quality to transmission fidelity. Third, civilisational and disciplinary history generates rich narrative without formal structure for testable predictions.

The decisive test is simple: can a given framework account for conditions in which a well-informed, analytically sound and properly authorised decision produces outcomes systematically opposite to those intended — that is, can it generate S < 0? The answer is uniformly NO across all traditions surveyed, except for Execution Economics.

On Scope: Execution Economics does not claim that adjacent traditions are wrong. Each illuminates one fragment of Y = d(P) × S while leaving the joint structure unformalised. The extended fields surveyed beyond economics — game theory, control theory, jurisprudence and others — encounter the same structural limit at their respective analytical frontiers. The missing variable is not absent from any one tradition; it is absent from the foundations shared across all of them.
