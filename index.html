Where the Framework Complements Existing Theories

A reading across 64 theoretical traditions — core formulas and variable definitions

Source corpus
Decision Sovereignty · Execution Economics · Measuring Decision Capability
Authors
Fritz, Fritz-Kalish & Bodrova
Families

FORMULA 1
CORE IDENTITY
Y = d(P) × S
Realised institutional output is the product of decision capability d(P) and decision sovereignty S. Where S = 0, no output is produced regardless of decision quality. Where S < 0, faithful and capable execution inverts the intended result.
FORMULA 2
DECISION CAPABILITY d(P)
Measuring Decision Capability (Fritz, Fritz-Kalish & Bodrova, 2026)
P = (D·Q·U·F)^(1/4)
d(P) = P^α
α ∈ (0, 1]
D
Data infrastructure — documented data availability, integration and accessibility within decision cycles. [0,1]
Q
Analytical quality — methodological sophistication with which data is converted into decisions. [0,1]
U
Updating from outcomes — documented capacity to close the loop between realised outcomes and future decisions. [0,1]
F
Forecast track record — documented accuracy of prior predictions against resolved outcomes. [0,1]
P
Predictive capacity — geometric mean of D, Q, U, F. Joint necessity: any component at zero drives P to zero. [0,1]
α
Cognitive exponent — quality degradation rate under decision overload. α = 1 at baseline; α → 0 as intensity rises. (0,1]
FORMULA 3
DECISION SOVEREIGNTY (canonical DSI)
Decision Sovereignty (Fritz, Fritz-Kalish & Bodrova, 2026)
DSI = ∛(A·C·E)·(1−V)
A, C, E, V ∈ [0,1]
DSI ∈ [0, 1]
A
Authority — legitimate and effective decision-making power. Real authority requires that others act on the decision-maker's choices. [0,1]
C
Control — operational capacity to implement decisions: resources, personnel, infrastructure. C = 0 renders perfect A and E inoperative. [0,1]
E
Execution-relevant information — usable information at the point of decision. Degrades at distance from operations and under high load. [0,1]
V
Veto exposure — degree to which actors can block or invert execution. Enters only as (1 − V): asymmetric, subtracts, never adds. V → 1 collapses DSI. [0,1]
∛
Cube-root complementarity — sovereignty collapses if any of A, C, E approaches zero; not an average but a joint function.
FORMULA 4
SIGNED SOVEREIGNTY (cosine alignment)
Net mission alignment via cosine similarity
A_T = cos(θ, φ)
= (θ·φ)/(∥θ∥·∥φ∥)
A_T ∈ [−1, +1]
θ
Intent vector — normalised representation of stated institutional mission.
φ
Execution vector — normalised representation of revealed institutional behaviour.
A_T
Cosine alignment — sign of A_T sets the sign of S; A_T < 0 is the structural reversal condition.
S
Signed sovereignty — canonical DSI scaled by A_T; enters Y = d(P) · S · T.
FORMULA 5
TRANSMISSION
multiplicative — supersedes the earlier min-function form
T = β·A_T·(1−χ)
χ(ρ) = 1/(1+e^(−γ(ρ−ρ_c)))
β
Baseline transmission capacity. [0,1]
A_T
Transmission alignment (cosine similarity, Formula 4). [−1, +1]
1 − χ
Congestion-free fraction; χ is the logistic congestion friction. [0,1]

The complementarity question

This document maps where the Execution Economics framework — built on the core identity Y = d(P) · S with sovereignty operationalised through DSI = ∛(A · C · E) · (1 − V), and signed via the cosine alignment A_T defined above — stands in relation to the principal theoretical traditions it draws on, refines, or extends.

The relationship is rarely replacement. In most cases EE supplies a missing variable (sovereignty, transmission), a missing functional form (the logistic congestion function, the geometric-mean DSI, the cosine alignment), or a missing scope condition that the host theory had implicitly assumed away.

The decisive test applied across 64 traditions is: can a given framework formally generate, as a structural variable in a production identity, conditions in which a well-informed, analytically sound and properly authorised decision produces outcomes systematically opposite to those intended? The answer is consistently no across the traditions surveyed, with the partial exception of constitutional theory (Madison, Federalist 51), which engineers veto to constrain the sign of S without formalising it. The argument is one of formal specificity, not of conceptual originality: substantive recognition of the gap between intent and outcome appears across many of these traditions, but the formal apparatus combining a signed transmission operator with a production identity is what the framework supplies.

Reading guide

The 52 traditions carried over from the source corpus are organised into 15 families (Core 1–6, 30 traditions; Extended 7–15, 22 traditions). To these, this document adds 9 traditions across 7 new families (16–22) for which EE has clear application: development economics, project management, regulatory science, corporate governance, monetary policy, public health, and AI safety. A further three traditions — Goodhart's Law, Campbell's Law, and Merton (1936) on unanticipated consequences — are added in Family 23 (Performativity and unanticipated consequences) because the survey would be incomplete without them. Total: 64 traditions across 23 families.

DSI components

  • A — Authority [0,1]
  • C — Control [0,1]
  • E — Execution-relevant information [0,1]
  • V — Veto exposure [0,1]
  • A_T — Transmission alignment (cosine similarity) [−1, +1]

Five regimes of S

  • High sovereignty: DSI ≥ 0.60
  • Robust: 0.40–0.60
  • Executable: 0.25–0.40
  • Weak / fragile: 0.01–0.25
  • Symbolic / reversal: ≤ 0.00 (signed via A_T < 0)

Family 1

Institutional and new institutional economics

4 traditions
Family 1 · INSTITUTIONAL AND NEW INSTITUTIONAL ECONOMICS Tradition 1.1

Transaction-cost economics

Coase (1937); Williamson (1985, 1996).

What the theory explains
Why firms exist; how transaction costs determine make-or-buy boundaries; how asset specificity shapes governance.
What it cannot explain
Treats decision authority as largely uncontested once firm boundaries are drawn; cannot explain why optimal Coasean boundaries still fail to execute. Williamson (1996) addresses some of this through forbearance and hierarchical decomposition but does not formalise transmission.
What EE adds
S = f(A, C, E, V)
Supplies signed sovereignty S decomposed into A (Authority), C (Control), E (Execution-relevant information) and V (Veto exposure). High transaction cost attenuates ∛(A · C · E) · (1 − V) but does not invert it; the inversion mode requires A_T < 0.
EE source documents
Execution Economics (2026 book) Ch. 2.1–2.2; Ch. 4.3 theoretical forebears.
Family 1 · INSTITUTIONAL AND NEW INSTITUTIONAL ECONOMICS Tradition 1.2

Property rights and institutional history

North (1990); Acemoglu and Robinson (2012); Acemoglu, Johnson and Robinson (2001).

What the theory explains
Why some societies sustain growth; inclusive vs extractive institutions; property-rights regimes and long-run outcomes.
What it cannot explain
Conflates decision quality with execution capacity. The inclusive vs extractive distinction is a design taxonomy, not a formal execution measure with a sign.
What EE adds
sign(S) via cosine alignment
Separates d(P) from S and signs S via A_T. Same DSI magnitude can carry opposite signs through the alignment vector; post-colonial states show inclusive design with low E and high V.
EE source documents
Execution Economics (2026 book) Ch. 2; Ch. 4.4.1; Ch. 6.
Family 1 · INSTITUTIONAL AND NEW INSTITUTIONAL ECONOMICS Tradition 1.3

Commons governance

Ostrom (1990); Hardin (1968).

What the theory explains
How shared resources are governed without privatisation or state control; design principles for self-organising communities.
What it cannot explain
Design principles are necessary preconditions for sovereignty but silent on congestion in shared decision channels.
What EE adds
χ(ρ) congestion friction
Extends to a tragedy of the decision commons: shared institutional channels overload, generating congestion friction χ(ρ) that compresses C and E inside the cube root of the DSI.
EE source documents
Execution Economics (2026 book) Ch. 5.4.2–5.4.3.
Family 1 · INSTITUTIONAL AND NEW INSTITUTIONAL ECONOMICS Tradition 1.4

Veto-player theory

Tsebelis (2002).

What the theory explains
How veto-player count and ideological distance shape policy stability and the probability of policy change.
What it cannot explain
Treats veto as an exogenous structural fact; does not specify the signed effect of veto accumulation on output direction.
What EE adds
(1 − V) multiplier
V enters as the (1 − V) multiplier on the cube root of (A · C · E), giving veto a proportional drag on the geometric mean of execution capacity rather than a simple subtraction. Endogenisation of V is not yet supplied — V is reformulated, not endogenised.

Family 2

Bounded rationality and behavioural economics

4 traditions
Family 2 · BOUNDED RATIONALITY AND BEHAVIOURAL ECONOMICS Tradition 2.1

Bounded rationality

Simon (1955, 1976); March and Simon (1958).

What the theory explains
Decision-makers satisfice rather than maximise; organisational processes differ from frictionless choice.
What it cannot explain
Identifies the cognitive mechanism without production-theoretic consequences; does not specify when satisficing inverts institutional output.
What EE adds
d(P) = P^α
Recasts as the decision-production function d(P) = P^α capturing degradation under load. The α exponent is governed by cognitive capacity and feeds into d(P) multiplied against ∛(A · C · E) · (1 − V).
EE source documents
Execution Economics (2026 book) Ch. 1.3.2; Ch. 1.4; Ch. 4.3.
Family 2 · BOUNDED RATIONALITY AND BEHAVIOURAL ECONOMICS Tradition 2.2

Heuristics, biases, prospect theory

Kahneman and Tversky (1979); Kahneman (2011).

What the theory explains
Systematic deviations: loss aversion, framing, anchoring; System 1 vs System 2 cognition.
What it cannot explain
Diagnoses individual-level deficiencies without connecting to institutional output; biases affect d(P) but the framework is silent on S.
What EE adds
α shift under load
Reads System 1/2 as a shift in α under cognitive load. System 1 dominance under institutional stress reduces d(P) and indirectly degrades S via governance curvature κ that attenuates C and E.
EE source documents
Execution Economics (2026 book) Ch. 3 the human factor; Ch. 4.3.
Family 2 · BOUNDED RATIONALITY AND BEHAVIOURAL ECONOMICS Tradition 2.3

Decision fatigue and the Yerkes–Dodson Law

Yerkes and Dodson (1908); ego-depletion literature, with replication considerations noted.

What the theory explains
Cognitive resources depleted by use; arousal-performance inverted-U; performance declines as stress rises beyond an optimum.
What it cannot explain
Individual-level phenomenon without an institutional aggregation model; cannot predict when accumulated fatigue produces sign reversal at system level.
What EE adds
S(L) = e^(−κL²)
Aggregates into governance curvature S(L) = e^(−κL²) at institutional scale, where L is decision intensity. The empirical anchoring should acknowledge the post-2016 replication discussion in the ego-depletion literature.
EE source documents
Execution Economics (2026 book) Ch. 3.2; Appendix notations.
Family 2 · BOUNDED RATIONALITY AND BEHAVIOURAL ECONOMICS Tradition 2.4

Garbage-can model

Cohen, March and Olsen (1972); March and Olsen (2010).

What the theory explains
High-volume decisions decouple from analysis and reflect coincidence, political negotiation and accident.
What it cannot explain
Descriptive; cannot distinguish random decoupling from systematic signed inversion of institutional intent.
What EE adds
α → 0 as ρ → ρ*
Formalises as α → 0 as decision volume rises relative to capacity, connecting to the congestion regime ρ > ρ*. Garbage-can dynamics are the overload limit of the EE production function.

Family 3

Organisational economics, contract theory, mechanism design

5 traditions
Family 3 · ORGANISATIONAL ECONOMICS, CONTRACT THEORY, MECHANISM DESIGN Tradition 3.1

Information economics and contract theory

Akerlof (1970); Holmström (1979); Holmström and Milgrom (1991); Stiglitz (2000).

What the theory explains
Asymmetric information shaping markets and contracts; signalling, screening; multitask principal-agent design.
What it cannot explain
Treats information as the primary friction and assumes authority is intact post-contract; does not address partial, contested or vetoed authority.
What EE adds
S as missing variable
Supplies the missing S. Even with perfectly aligned incentives (Holmström optimum), if (1 − V) → 0 then DSI → 0 and Y collapses regardless of d(P). Incentive alignment is not transmission fidelity.
EE source documents
Execution Economics (2026 book) I.2.2; Ch. 5.2.
Family 3 · ORGANISATIONAL ECONOMICS, CONTRACT THEORY, MECHANISM DESIGN Tradition 3.2

Principal–agent theory

Holmström (1979); Hart and successors.

What the theory explains
Divergence between principal and agent objectives under asymmetric information; alignment via incentive design.
What it cannot explain
Even when incentives are aligned and decisions are implemented as intended, outcomes can still diverge. Operates on alignment of decisions rather than on transmission of effects.
What EE adds
Signed transmission via A_T
Provides the signed transmission variable that closes the gap. Robodebt-class cases show faithful execution producing inverted outcomes: high d(P) and aligned incentives, yet ∛(A · C · E) · (1 − V) carrying a negative net sign through A_T.
EE source documents
Execution Economics (2026 book) Ch. 5.2; Ch. 5.4; Ch. 6.3; Ch. 18.
Family 3 · ORGANISATIONAL ECONOMICS, CONTRACT THEORY, MECHANISM DESIGN Tradition 3.3

Organisational architecture and formal vs real authority

Milgrom and Roberts (1990); Aghion and Tirole (1997).

What the theory explains
Decision-rights allocation, incentive configuration, complementarities; the gap between formal and real authority.
What it cannot explain
Assumes that once allocation is correct, implementation follows. Formal authority A on its own is not decision sovereignty.
What EE adds
A inside ∛(A · C · E)
A is one of three positive components inside the cube root. The Aghion–Tirole formal/real gap is decomposed: formal authority absent C or E still yields low DSI even before V is applied.
EE source documents
Execution Economics (2026 book) Ch. 1.4; Ch. 2.2.3.
Family 3 · ORGANISATIONAL ECONOMICS, CONTRACT THEORY, MECHANISM DESIGN Tradition 3.4

O-ring theory

Kremer (1993).

What the theory explains
Weakest-link production; quality complementarities; assortative matching; small task-quality differences generate large output differences.
What it cannot explain
Formally similar to EE’s weakest-link transmission structure but only for positive output; cannot represent reversal when the weakest link turns hostile.
What EE adds
Signed O-ring extension
EE absorbs Kremer’s weakest-link structure into the cube-root composite (each of A, C, E behaves O-ring-like) and adds a signed extension under which mission-extractive components invert rather than merely reduce output.
EE source documents
Execution Economics (2026 book) Ch. 7.4.3.
Family 3 · ORGANISATIONAL ECONOMICS, CONTRACT THEORY, MECHANISM DESIGN Tradition 3.5

Mechanism design and Pigovian pricing

Pigou (1920); Hurwicz (1973); Maskin (1999); Maskin and Tirole (1999); Laffont and Tirole (1993).

What the theory explains
Internalising externalities through prices and mechanisms; regulator incentive design; conditions for implementability.
What it cannot explain
Implementation theory is binary — a mechanism either yields the intended outcome or it does not. Specifies conditions under which outcomes can be achieved but not the fidelity with which they are preserved in execution.
What EE adds
Continuous signed fidelity
Replaces binary implementability with continuous signed fidelity ∛(A · C · E) · (1 − V) · sign(A_T). Identifies cases where institutional architecture prevents an otherwise-implementable mechanism from operating, locating the failure in V and E rather than in the mechanism itself.
EE source documents
Execution Economics (2026 book) Ch. 5.2; Ch. 5.4.3.

Operations research, queueing theory, management practices

6 traditions
Family 4 · OPERATIONS RESEARCH, QUEUEING THEORY, MANAGEMENT PRACTICES Tradition 4.1

Queueing theory

Kleinrock (1975); Little’s law and successors.

What the theory explains
Non-linear waiting times as utilisation approaches capacity; service disciplines; convex congestion.
What it cannot explain
Fixed institutional structure with no alignment or sovereignty layer; models delay but not inversion of throughput direction.
What EE adds
Logistic χ(ρ)
Imports queueing dynamics into the congestion regime via the logistic specification χ(ρ) = 1 / (1 + exp(−γ(ρ − ρ_c))). Adds the signed sovereignty layer: even uncongested systems can deliver Y < 0 when A_T is hostile.
EE source documents
Execution Economics (2026 book) Ch. 5.1.2–5.1.3; Ch. 10.2.3.
Family 4 · OPERATIONS RESEARCH, QUEUEING THEORY, MANAGEMENT PRACTICES Tradition 4.2

Operations research and linear programming

Dantzig (1947); general optimisation tradition.

What the theory explains
Optimisation within constraints: scheduling, routing, pricing, staffing; finding the feasible optimum.
What it cannot explain
Presumes the structural capacity to implement the optimal solution. The constraint set is exogenous; OR does not explain how constraints arise or differ across organisations.
What EE adds
Endogenous constraints
Endogenises the constraint set. The OR feasible region corresponds to the region where ∛(A · C · E) · (1 − V) > 0 with A_T > 0; outside it, the optimal solution cannot be transmitted regardless of mathematical correctness.
EE source documents
Execution Economics (2026 book) P.2 scope, claims and non-claims.
Family 4 · OPERATIONS RESEARCH, QUEUEING THEORY, MANAGEMENT PRACTICES Tradition 4.3

Management practices empirics

Bloom and Van Reenen (2007); Bloom, Eifert, Mahajan, McKenzie and Roberts (2013).

What the theory explains
Cross-country and cross-firm management-practice variation correlated with productivity.
What it cannot explain
Unsigned quality scores. Cannot diagnose reversal: mission-extractive institutions can score high on management quality.
What EE adds
Magnitude × sign decomposition
Adds the sign(S) decomposition. World Management Survey scores measure ∛(A · C · E) magnitude well; EE supplies (1 − V) and the alignment direction A_T that determine whether a high-scoring firm produces Y > 0 or Y < 0.

Execution Economics (2026 book) Ch. 4.4.2; Ch. 10.1.
Family 4 · OPERATIONS RESEARCH, QUEUEING THEORY, MANAGEMENT PRACTICES Tradition 4.4

Governance indicators

Kaufmann, Kraay and Mastruzzi (2010), Worldwide Governance Indicators.

What the theory explains
Cross-national scoring across voice, political stability, government effectiveness, regulatory quality, rule of law and corruption.
What it cannot explain
Unsigned and aggregated at national level; cannot distinguish mission-aligned from mission-extractive institutional capacity at the programme level. The Robodebt scheme operated within a high-scoring national governance environment yet produced structurally inverted outcomes.
What EE adds
Signed sovereignty product
Provides a signed sovereignty product. Same national-level governance data, opposite programme-level diagnoses: WGI reads high; ∛(A · C · E) · (1 − V) under EE diagnoses the Robodebt regime as deeply negative because the alignment vector A_T was hostile to the stated policy intent.

Execution Economics (2026 book) Ch. 4.4.1; Ch. 18.4; Ch. 18 Robodebt.
Family 4 · OPERATIONS RESEARCH, QUEUEING THEORY, MANAGEMENT PRACTICES Tradition 4.5

Dynamic capabilities theory

Teece, Pisano and Shuen (1997).

What the theory explains
Capacity of firms to integrate, build and reconfigure competences in response to changing conditions.
What it cannot explain
Possessing a capability is not the same as producing the intended result. Establishes that a firm can act, but does not specify whether the effect aligns with intended direction.
What EE adds
S as signed product
Capabilities populate A and C inside the cube root but do not determine sign. EE supplies the ∛(A · C · E) · (1 − V) signed product (with A_T) that converts a possessed capability into realised outcome.

Execution Economics (2026 book) Ch. 5.2.
Family 4 · OPERATIONS RESEARCH, QUEUEING THEORY, MANAGEMENT PRACTICES Tradition 4.6

Evolutionary and absorptive-capacity theory

Nelson and Winter (1982); Cohen and Levinthal (1990).

What the theory explains
How organisational routines evolve, are selected, and how firms process and apply external knowledge over time.
What it cannot explain
Captures variation, selection and learning within organisations. Does not distinguish routines that preserve the intended effect of a decision from those that systematically alter or invert it.
What EE adds
Signed alignment over routines
Routines feed into C and E inside the cube root. EE adds the signed alignment that determines whether evolved routines preserve, attenuate or invert a faithfully executed decision.

Family 5 · CIVILISATIONAL HISTORY AND LONG-RUN POLITICAL DEVELOPMENT Tradition 5.1

Geographic determinism

Diamond (1997).

What the theory explains
Ecological endowments shape the deep distribution of civilisations and the speed of pre-modern development.
What it cannot explain
Explains pre-modern distribution but not modern outcomes. Resource-poor states with strong execution outperform resource-rich states with weak execution.
What EE adds
Carrier variable
Geography sets the stage; it does not write the play. EE supplies the institutional carrier ∛(A · C · E) · (1 − V) through which geographic endowments are or are not converted into realised output.

Family 5 · CIVILISATIONAL HISTORY AND LONG-RUN POLITICAL DEVELOPMENT Tradition 5.2

Cognitive and ideational history

Harari (2014); McCloskey (2010).

What the theory explains
Shared fictions enable cooperation; ideational origins of modern growth.
What it cannot explain
Treats ideas as causally primary. The scientific method, advances at Maragha and the House of Wisdom in 9th-century Baghdad, and movable type in Song China were available; they did not move the world until they met execution systems.
What EE adds
S as transmission carrier
Supplies the carrier: ∛(A · C · E) · (1 − V) determines whether an idea propagates into institutional output. Same idea, different DSI, different civilisational outcome.

State-formation theory

Fukuyama (2011); Besley and Persson (2011).

What the theory explains
How merit-recruited bureaucracy emerged; the Chinese precedence; the bureaucracy–rule-of-law–accountability triad.

Reads achievement as a developmental endpoint rather than as specific sovereignty arrangements. Cannot explain why well-formed states still fail to execute major reforms.
What EE adds
K = K₀ · Q^φ · D^ψ
Reads Fukuyama’s structures as sovereignty arrangements that propagate for structural reasons. EE formalises propagation as the institutional capacity equation K = K₀ · Q^φ · D^ψ feeding into C and E inside the cube root.

Family 5 · CIVILISATIONAL HISTORY AND LONG-RUN POLITICAL DEVELOPMENT Tradition 5.4

Innovation clustering and incumbent failure

Christensen (1997); Perez (2002).

What the theory explains
Why incumbents fail to adopt disruptive technologies; technological revolutions arrive in regular surges and require institutional re-institutionalisation.
What it cannot explain
Christensen attributes incumbent failure to unwillingness to cannibalise margins, not to endogenous decision-governance limits. Perez identifies the lag between technological surge and institutional adjustment but does not formalise the transmission constraint.
What EE adds
Convex governance costs
Locates incumbent failure in convex governance costs that compress ∛(A · C · E) · (1 − V) as decision intensity rises. Re-institutionalisation is the period during which V is renegotiated and E rebuilt around the new technology.

Family 5 · CIVILISATIONAL HISTORY AND LONG-RUN POLITICAL DEVELOPMENT Tradition 5.5

Neolithic materialism

Childe (1936); Henrich (2004).

What the theory explains
Agricultural transition transformed social organisation; cultural transmission scales with population size and connectivity.
What it cannot explain
Childe’s causal direction is contested by parts of the recent archaeological record (Göbekli Tepe debates remain active). Henrich’s framework has the transmission mathematics but no explicit institutional carrier.
What EE adds
Institutional carrier
Absorbs the transmission mathematics and supplies an institutional carrier: ∛(A · C · E) · (1 − V) is the precondition for agricultural and technological transitions, with the causal direction read as institution-leading rather than institution-following.

Religious sociology

Durkheim (1912); Weber (1905).

Family 6 · SOCIOLOGY OF INSTITUTIONS, COLLECTIVE ACTION AND IMPLEMENTATION STUDIES Tradition 6.3

Collective action and free-riding

Olson (1965).

What the theory explains
Concentrated costs and diffuse benefits create obstacles to reform; why groups often fail to act in their collective interest.
What it cannot explain
Explains why reform is hard, not how transmission fidelity is engineered when reform succeeds. Does not formalise the S of successful collective action.
What EE adds
Execution complement
Supplements with the structural variable for execution once the collective-action problem is solved. Overcoming free-riding raises A but is necessary, not sufficient, for ∛(A · C · E) · (1 − V) > 0.

Network sociology

Granovetter (1973).

Network × S = Y
Provides the institutional structure that determines whether network information translates into executed decisions. Network topology populates E at the point of action; ∛(A · C · E) · (1 − V) governs whether that information is acted upon.

Family 6 · SOCIOLOGY OF INSTITUTIONS, COLLECTIVE ACTION AND IMPLEMENTATION STUDIES Tradition 6.5

Decentralised knowledge

Hayek (1945).

What the theory explains
Dispersed knowledge that cannot be centralised; the price system as an information-aggregating mechanism.
What it cannot explain
Shows why central planning fails; not when decentralised execution succeeds. No formal model of execution-relevant information in decentralised settings.

Cross-cutting observations

Three structural patterns

First, EE rarely contradicts host theories on their own terms. It identifies a variable they hold fixed (typically S, decomposed into A, C, E inside the cube root and V outside it, signed by the cosine alignment A_T) and lets it vary, then reads the result.

Second, EE imports several functional forms wholesale rather than inventing new mathematics where existing forms suffice: Cobb–Douglas (Solow, Romer) for the institutional capacity equation K = K₀ · Q^φ · D^ψ; cosine similarity (text analysis and elsewhere in social science) for A_T; the logistic congestion curve χ(ρ) (queueing theory); the geometric mean for the symmetric cube-root composite over A, C, E; and the exponential governance-curvature decay S(L) = e^(−κL²).

Third, the framework is most distinctive on what it adds where existing literatures are silent: the asymmetric structural role of veto in the canonical DSI (V enters only as the (1 − V) multiplier on the cube-rooted geometric mean of A, C and E); the explicit reversal condition under negative cosine alignment; and the separation of decision sovereignty from transmission.

The convergent-impossibility argument, restated

The S < 0 column records whether a tradition contains some mechanism by which negative outcomes can arise — not whether it formally specifies structural reversal as a first-class variable in a production identity. On this reading, a number of traditions are marked Yes because they acknowledge failure modes, misalignment, or capture that can produce results opposite to those intended. Constitutional theory is marked Implicit because Madison's design intentionally engineers V to constrain the sign of S — the closest any prior framework comes to specifying a signed transmission operator, though without formalising it.

The argument operates at a more specific level: no tradition surveyed independently formalises a signed transmission operator inside a production identity for realised institutional output. Traditions that acknowledge negative outcomes do so through auxiliary narratives (misaligned incentives, capture, veto-player gridlock, target distortion, unanticipated consequences) rather than through a production identity in which the sovereignty term carries a sign that can flip. The argument is therefore one of formal specificity, not conceptual originality. The case rests on the cumulative weight of the survey at this level of formal specificity, and the framework should be presented as supplying a formalisation rather than discovering a missing variable.

What the tables do not contain

EE explicitly disclaims certain domains. It is not a theory of management consulting or operations research; it concerns the structural environment within which those operate. It is silent on the normative question of what decisions should be made.

Practitioner literatures (Bossidy and Charan, Execution; McChesney, Covey and Huling, The 4 Disciplines of Execution; Pfeffer and Sutton, The Knowing-Doing Gap; Kotter, Leading Change; Mintzberg and colleagues, Strategy Safari; Waterman, Adhocracy; Ertell, The Strategy Trap, 2026) describe the same phenomenon at the management-advice level; EE provides the production-function expression these literatures lack but does not displace their practical recommendations.

EE also rejects the Schumpeterian framing in which incumbent failure is read as creative destruction by an exogenous innovator. Under EE, firms and organisations do not die because a more creative entity replaces them, but because they lack the structural decision sovereignty to support the activity they have committed to. The mechanism is internal — convex governance costs, compressed E and rising V — not the arrival of a competitor.
